Volatility Time Calculation

Trading Day Time​

In an earlier version of the SpiderRock platform, the calculation for time-to-expiration followed a business-day count convention (BUS/252) with intraday time decay. As time progressed over a trading session, the time-to-expiration would decay each minute, starting at a full fraction of a day upon market open and decaying to zero by the end of the day. The rule-of-thumb for calculating time was to start with the number of days left to expiration (including the current day), and subtract the fraction of the trading day that had elapsed since the market opened. This fraction was calculated by taking the time elapsed between 9:30 AM and 4:00 PM ET and dividing by 6.5 (the total hours occuring from market open to close).

However, this approach oversimplifies the relationship between the underlying price movement during the trading session, and after the market is closed. It assumes that no volatility occurs unless the market is open, and it ignores the possibility that a contract might have trading activity outside of these hours.

Volatility Time​

In SpiderRock Connect, the latest version of SpiderRock's platform, the time decay has been updated to account for volatility that occurs both during and after the trading session. For US equity markets, roughly 70% of the price variance occurs during market trading hours and additionally during the hour immediately after market close. To better account for this relationship in a business-day count convention, we distinguish between higher volatility and lower volatility intervals of time throughout an entire trading year. For sake of simplicity, we will refer to the higher volatility intervals as trading hours and the lower volatility intervals as non-trading hours.

Consider an option expiring exactly 48 hours from now, after two consecutive days of trading. Suppose that a typical interval of trading hour time is 8 hours long, then there is twice as much time spent in non-trading hours than is spent in trading hours (i.e. 16 hours off, then 8 hours on). If the forecast volatility used to price the option is 20%, but 70% of the price variance is expected to occur during two 8-hour trading sessions, then the time decay can be shifted to make the total variance accrue at a constant rate:

Note

The two plots actually represent the same relationship between time and volatility. The difference comes from a change in perspective, between the actual hours that pass and the time inferred from an assumption of constant variance. In order to make volatility decay at a constant rate, the "volatility time" needs to reflect the fraction of variance which occurs over each period of time. Simply put, if the non-trading hours generate 30% of the variance, then the volatility time for that period should be 0.3 days.

Extending this concept to a business-day count convention for equities, suppose there are 252 trading days, each with 7.5 hours of trading time, representing a total of [252 days * 7.5 hours = 1,890 hours.] Under a 252-trading-day year assumption, the total number of hours in a year is [365 days * 24 hours = 8,760 hours], so the remaining non-trading hours in the year amount to [8,760 total hours - 1,890 trading hours = 6,870 non-trading hours]. If expected volatility is the same between market and non-market hours, each hour should count as 1/8,760 of a year.

In this constant volatility framework, the entire year can be broken up into 1638 trading and 6,870 non-trading time segments, each representing an hour, all of which add up to 1:

1,890×18,760+6,870×18,760=11,890 \times \frac{1}{8,760} + 6,870 \times \frac{1}{8,760} = 11,890×8,7601​+6,870×8,7601​=1

However, in a business day count convention, where the 6,870 non-trading hours account for none of the variance, the time unit used for a single trading hour needs to be adjusted so that the time measured during each trading session accounts for all of the time accumulated in the year. This is accomplished by counting all non-trading hours as equal to zero and counting each trading hour as 1/1,890 of a year:

1,890×11,890+6,870×0=11,890 \times \frac{1}{1,890} + 6,870 \times 0 = 11,890×1,8901​+6,870×0=1

Note

When volatility time uses 1/1,890 for each trading hour, the time elapsed during open market hours is 7.5/1,890 years, which is equal to 1/252.

The generalization is fairly straightforward. If you want trading time to account for 70% of the variance, then the first term in the equation (i.e. total trading hour time) needs to be 0.7, and the second term needs to be 0.3:

1,890×0.71,890+6,870×0.36,870=11,890 \times \frac{0.7}{1,890} + 6,870 \times \frac{0.3}{6,870} = 11,890×1,8900.7​+6,870×6,8700.3​=1

Any weight can be used to determine the volatility time for a trading hour and for a non-trading hour, by taking a weighted average where α is the percent of volatility attributed to trading time:

1,890×α1,890+6,870×1−α6,870=11,890 \times \frac{α}{1,890} + 6,870 \times \frac{1 - α}{6,870} = 11,890×1,890α​+6,870×6,8701−α​=1

With this approach, we account for time in terms of fractions of hours during trading and fractions of hours outside of trading, each multiplied by their respective volatility “times” for a trading market hour and a non-trading market hour.

Time to Expiration=(Trading Hours Remaining)×α1,890+(NonTrading Hours Remaining)×1−α6,870Time \space to \space Expiration = (Trading \space Hours \space Remaining) \times \frac{α}{1,890} + (NonTrading \space Hours \space Remaining) \times \frac{1-α}{6,870}Time to Expiration=(Trading Hours Remaining)×1,890α​+(NonTrading Hours Remaining)×6,8701−α​
